> When your dashboard shouts "$150,000 in sales this month", how much actually stays in your pocket? Way less than you think.

## The "gross sales" problem

Most analytics tools (GA4, MonsterInsights, the default WooCommerce dashboard) display **gross revenue**. It's the total amount paid by customers.

Typical example:

```
Gross revenue this month: $150,000
```

Sounds great. But that number hides:
- Taxes you owe to the government (not your money)
- Shipping fees paid by customers (pass-through to carriers)
- Stripe / PayPal transaction fees (2.9% + $0.30 per transaction)
- Refunds (you have to give them back)
- Cost of goods sold (COGS)
- Discounts applied

Once all that is subtracted, your **real revenue** can be 40-60% lower.

## What Ajnix displays

For every WooCommerce order, Ajnix captures and displays 7 dimensions:

| Dimension | Example | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Gross** | $150 | What the customer pays (WC total) |
| **Tax** | $22.50 | Applicable taxes (not yours) |
| **Shipping** | $12 | Shipping fees (pass-through) |
| **Fees** | $4.65 | Payment fees (Stripe, PayPal, etc.) |
| **Discount** | $15 | Discounts / coupons applied |
| **Refunded** | $0 | Refunded amounts (if applicable) |
| **COGS** | $45 | Cost of goods |
| **Net** | $51.85 | What actually stays with you |

**Net** is computed automatically:
```
Net = Gross - Tax - Shipping - Fees - Refunded - COGS
```

<Callout type="note">
You can configure COGS per product in **Ajnix > Costs > Product COGS**. Without COGS, Ajnix will still compute net without deducting material costs, but will flag that the figure is incomplete.
</Callout>

## Why it changes your decisions

### Concrete example

Imagine two acquisition channels:

```
Channel A (Google Ads):
- Gross revenue: $50,000
- Acquisition cost: $8,000
- Apparent "ROAS": 6.25x
```

Looks great. But if we look at real net revenue:

```
Channel A (Google Ads):
- Gross revenue: $50,000
- Taxes (non-recoverable, pass-through): $7,500
- Shipping (pass-through): $4,000
- Payment fees (3%): $1,500
- COGS: $15,000
- Discounts applied: $3,000
- Net revenue: $19,000
- Acquisition cost: $8,000
- "True ROAS": 2.38x
```

Drops from 6.25x to 2.38x. If you optimize on apparent ROAS, you'll overinvest in channels that are actually unprofitable.

### With another channel

```
Channel B (Email marketing):
- Gross revenue: $30,000
- Acquisition cost: $500 (email tool)
- Net revenue: $14,000
- True ROAS: 28x
```

Email marketing often looks like a "small channel" in gross reports, but its true ROAS crushes everything else because acquisition costs are near zero.

<Callout type="warning">
Without a revenue breakdown, you're making decisions on lying numbers. That's the main reason so many e-commerce brands burn Google Ads or Meta Ads budget that's actually unprofitable.
</Callout>

## Refunds

Ajnix tracks refunds as compensating events. When you refund an order:

- The `refund` event is created automatically via the WooCommerce `woocommerce_order_refunded` hook
- The refunded amount is subtracted from the period's net revenue
- The Ecommerce dashboard displays refunds separately for analysis

<Callout type="tip">
A channel with lots of refunds (e.g., TikTok Ads bringing impulse buyers who regret it) naturally looks less profitable in Ajnix, even if gross revenue is impressive. That's a feature, not a bug.
</Callout>

## Transaction fees

Ajnix auto-detects payment fees for the major gateways:

- Stripe (via WooCommerce Stripe plugin)
- PayPal (via WooCommerce PayPal Payments plugin)
- Square
- Others: configurable via the `ajnix_transaction_fee_meta_keys` filter

Fees are stored per order and aggregated in ROI reports.
